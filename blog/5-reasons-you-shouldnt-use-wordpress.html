5 reasons you shouldn’t use WordPress

Joshua Matambo
|
September 15, 2020

For a long time, WordPress was the easiest, fastest, and most convenient way for a business to set up a website. That is why about 30% of all websites are made using WordPress, but as you know with technology, they are always something newer and improved, which in this case would be Webflow. I am going to list reasons down below why you should not use WordPress:

‍
One of the most important things you want your website to be is secure, since millions of websites use WordPress, they are prime targets for hackers. If a hacker finds a vulnerability on one website, chances are very high that that same vulnerability can be found on many other websites. If your website is not managed and updated regularly, it is only a matter of time before your website is hacked into and repurposed.

‍
WordPress comes with default designs called themes, this help speed up the website's development time, but since millions of websites use these same themes, they tend to look somewhat similar to each other. Themes also limit the creator's creativity to what is allowed by the theme since they are not very customizable.
WordPress breaks down too frequently. WordPress is designed so loosely that all the pieces, plugins and technology fall apart frequently. You'll need to hire a WordPress developer every couple of months to make sure it's all still working correctly, this can become expensive very quickly, and that's not including any website changes, improvements or modifications as your business grows. WordPress is not cost-effective for small businesses.

‍
WordPress is too expensive to manage. Once you get your website up in WordPress, get ready for a world of hurt. WordPress sites often break down every couple of months. It needs to be continuously fixed and updated as online technology changes. Be prepared to hire multiple WordPress developers to fix your site until you get tired of WordPress altogether and either decide to leave WordPress or not have a website at all.

‍
The speed of a website affects the SEO as well as the general user experience. As WordPress caters for many different styles of websites and has lots of features that are often unused, the code is very 'bloated'. This means your server is processing a lot more code than it needs to, which means each page is slower.
